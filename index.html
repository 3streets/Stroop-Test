The briefing

One simple instruction

You'll be shown a word, printed in coloured ink. Your job is to name the colour of the ink — and to completely ignore what the word actually spells.

It sounds trivial. It is not. When the word and the ink disagree, something in your mind resists, and that resistance is measurable down to the millisecond.

The science

Why your reading brain wins

For a literate adult, reading is not a choice. After years of practice it has become automatic — it happens the instant your eyes land on a word, with no permission required. Naming a colour, by contrast, is a slower, more deliberate act.

When the word and the ink disagree, two answers arrive at once — and your mind must spend time throwing the wrong one away.

That discarding takes time. The gap between your two rounds — your interference score — is a direct measurement of how long your brain needed to suppress the automatic reader and let the deliberate colour-namer through. Psychologists call this response conflict, and it is handled largely by the same frontal-lobe machinery you use for focus and self-control.

The effect is also asymmetric: if the task were reversed — read the word, ignore the colour — the conflicting ink would barely slow you at all. Reading overpowers colour-naming, but not the other way around. That one-way street is the real signature of an automatic process.
